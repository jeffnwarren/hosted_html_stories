THE CHALLENGE AND TRIAL OF THE MASTERS

An Endless Quest™-style Adventure

You are Karou, a fighting monk of the Jade Fist temple — three years into your training under the great Master Yenri. You have studied the ancient forms, learned to move like water and strike like stone, practiced until catching a thrown blade from the air is no more remarkable than catching a leaf.

Now Sir Brenin has come to your temple with desperate news. The city of Aldenmoor has fallen under the shadow of Xarak: an evil master in black whose students have broken every knight and soldier who stood against them. The city’s people live in fear. Your mentor, Master Yenri, went to negotiate three weeks ago and has not been heard from since.

Only another master of the fighting arts can stop Xarak. You are not a master yet — only Yenri can make you one. But you may be the closest thing left.

The choices are yours. Choose wisely — in Xarak’s city, strength alone will not save you.

1

The morning practice yard is still cool when Sir Brenin arrives. You are moving through the fifth form — the Crane’s Wing, slow and precise — when you hear horses at the gate and the clatter of armor.

Brenin is a big man, mustachioed, wearing a dusty surcoat over battered plate. His squire, a nervous boy half his age, reaches for a knife when he sees the monks watching from the walls — and throws it, pure reflex, straight at your chest.

You catch it. Left hand, without thinking, the blade flat between your fingers. The squire goes white. Brenin stares.

“Forgive the lad,” Brenin says quietly. “He was startled.” He looks at the knife in your hand and something settles in his expression — relief, perhaps, or the end of a long doubt. “I have come a long way to find someone who can do that.”

Master Yenri summons you an hour later. Brenin tells his story over cold tea: the city of Aldenmoor, his ancestral home, seized by a monk named Xarak who arrived a year ago with a dozen followers. Six months of resistance — knights, soldiers, two attempts at hired swords — all broken. Xarak’s students fight in ways that conventional armor cannot answer. And three weeks ago, Yenri himself went to Aldenmoor to negotiate, and has not been heard from since.

Yenri gives you a staff of ironwood, worn smooth by forty years of handling. “I was saving this conversation for your fifth year,” he says. “It seems the world has other plans. Go. What you know will either be enough, or it will not. You will not learn the difference standing here.”

Brenin is ready to leave at once. But you have heard of something: Aldric, a hermit wizard who lives in the ruins at Thornmere, two days east. He spent decades studying the ancient fighting orders — the same lineage Xarak claims. If anyone knows Xarak’s weaknesses, it is Aldric.

Three days of riding. The first two pass pleasantly enough — countryside, villages, and Brenin telling stories of Aldenmoor before Xarak. By the third day the villages are quieter and the roads emptier. People watch you pass from doorways without speaking.

Three shapes emerge from the dark. Black masks, black robes, moving with the particular economy of people who have trained for years. One has Brenin’s sword arm pinned before he can stand. The other two come for you.

The firelight catches the brass rings on the nearest monk’s fingers. He is watching your hands, your feet, your center of gravity. He knows the forms. These are not soldiers playing at fighting. These are Xarak’s students.

You shout one word to Brenin — “Down!” — and step between him and the nearest monk, staff horizontal in both hands.

The first monk comes with a spinning kick aimed at your head. You duck under it, catch his ankle on the way past, and redirect his momentum into the ground. Hard. He does not get up quickly.

The second is faster — he feints low and strikes high. You take a glancing blow on the shoulder that will bruise, but you use the impact to turn, and your elbow finds his ribs. He backs off, gasping.

The third has Brenin by the collar. You shout again; he looks up for just a moment, and Brenin headbutts him and breaks free.

The leader with the brass rings circles you last. He is the best of them. You fight him cleanly for two minutes, technique against technique, and you win by doing something unexpected: you stop entirely, wait, and let him come to you. He over-commits. You catch the strike at the wrist, bend it precisely, and he kneels.

Searching the leader’s kit afterward, you find a clay tablet tucked inside his robe: a character you recognize — “still water” — carved above a diagram of a cistern. You cannot read all of it, but you take it. It might mean something.
