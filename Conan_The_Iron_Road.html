CONAN: THE IRON ROAD

An Endless Quest Adventure

You are Conan of Cimmeria — seventeen years old, lean as wire, and two weeks clear of a Hyperborean slave camp. The witch Vammatar the Deathless owned you once. She means to own you again. Her war wolves are on your trail and her Marsh Rider allies are closing the road south.

Between you and the safety of Zamora: a hundred miles of hostile country, the haunted Black City of Wizards, a rain-swollen river, and an alliance of killers who have your description.

The choices ahead of you are real, and some of them will get you killed. Move fast. Think ahead. Trust your Cimmerian instincts — they have kept you alive this long.

1

The fire you built is small and smokeless, the way your father taught you. You are Conan of Cimmeria, and you have been running for fourteen days.

Before that: three months in a Hyperborean slave camp, hauling stone in the cold dark above the Arctic Circle, collared like a dog. You got out the hard way — through a gate you pried open with a stone chisel and across thirty miles of tundra in a blizzard. You have not stopped moving since. The witch Vammatar the Deathless owned the camp. She will want you back.

You are seventeen years old and you weigh your chances the way a Cimmerian does: clearly, without flinching.

You dream, as you do most nights now. A woman's face — white as birch bark, with eyes like green fire. She points at you across a great distance. She smiles. You know her name without being told. Vammatar.

You wake in the dark before dawn to the sound of war wolves howling on the ridge above your camp. They are big and they have your scent and they are perhaps two hours behind you. You break camp in the dark and start moving.

By first light you are in rough hill country above the Zamoran road, and the terrain ahead splits three ways.

The pines are dark and cold, the trunks close enough that the war wolves will have to pick their way single-file. That evens the odds. You push south at a loping run, following deer paths, your breath a steady white plume in the chill air.

Twenty minutes in, you nearly put your foot through a flat stone slab half-buried in pine needles. You stop.

Old stonework — notched and stacked granite, the style your father's people used for their warrior-dead. A Cimmerian cairn, far from home. You clear the creep of moss from the face-stone out of respect, and then you see it: a sword laid in the crevice behind the marker, wrapped in treated leather that has hardened with age but not rotted. The blade is iron, heavy, old-fashioned — a hand-and-a-half grip, plain crossguard, no ornament. You test the edge on your thumb. It still holds.

Whoever rests here left this for whoever came next.

You take the sword, sling it across your back, and push on until the trees thin and the Zamoran road appears below in the grey morning light.

The road is old Aquilonian military stone — wide, flat, nearly smooth despite a century of disuse. You make good time, long-striding it south with your pack riding high and, if you passed through the forest, the old Cimmerian sword across your back.

You approach across the open rock with your hands visible — a Cimmerian habit. Better to be seen than thought to be sneaking.

The woman watches you come without moving. Up close she is everything the distance suggested: seven feet tall or close to it, in battered silver-and-black plate that bears no maker's mark you recognize, with hair the color of aged iron and eyes like the grey space between a lightning strike and its thunder. At her hip, a sword whose pommel stone is carved with a rune you have never seen.

"You are the one Vammatar wants," she says. It is not a question.

"I am," you say. "And you?"

"I am called Rann." She studies you with an appraiser's calm. "I was asked to look at you by parties who do not give names freely." A pause. "There is an Aquilonian knight dying on the road below. He carries something that should not be left with a dead man. And hear this, Cimmerian: Vammatar's power over the mortal world is anchored in a ritual staff — black iron, carved with her runes. Her ability to follow you south, to manifest in flesh, depends on it. Destroy the staff and she cannot pursue you for years."

She turns away, as if the conversation is finished.

You go down to the road.

The war wolves find you at dusk, in a narrow gully where the road cuts between two rock faces. There are six of them — each one the size of a large pony, bred for this work, tireless. The Marsh Rider who follows them does not even dismount to watch.

The River Ossar in spring flood is nothing like the lazy brown stream it becomes in summer. Here it runs brown and fast, chest-deep at the ford, carrying branches and other debris at alarming speed. The footing on the river stones would be uncertain even without a full pack and the Marsh Riders a mile behind you.

You stand at the bank and weigh your options. Downstream, perhaps a quarter mile, an old mill sits on the south bank. Its wheel is still, but the bridge that served it is intact — barely. The planking looks rotten in places, and the railing has long since collapsed, but it spans the river completely.

The wolves are close enough now that you can hear them crashing through the undergrowth on the north bank. Minutes, not hours.

The track west deteriorates within a mile, then disappears altogether. You navigate by the dark smudge on the horizon that slowly resolves into towers — black, impossibly slender, rising against the grey sky with no apparent logic of architecture. The Black City of Wizards.

Up close, the outer wall is ancient stone that absorbs light rather than reflects it. Graffiti has been scratched into the facing stones over the centuries, in a dozen languages. Most of it says some version of the same thing: Turn back. One inscription, in old Aquilonian, reads: The gate opens inward. What comes through does not.

You walk the wall's perimeter, looking for options. The main gate is a massive iron arch, sealed but not locked — a bar holds it shut from the inside, somehow, though there is no evidence of recent habitation. Fifty yards west of the gate, a section of wall has partially collapsed, leaving a gap wide enough to squeeze through. Beyond the gap: dead streets, broken statues of things that were never human, and silence so complete it has a texture.

The gate opens easily when you lift the bar — too easily. Something on the inside wanted it opened.

The buildings are intact but wrong in their proportions — doors too narrow, windows placed at heights that suggest the original inhabitants were neither standing nor sitting when they looked out. The streets all look the same in the dark, and the ones that looked like south-facing passages turn and double back without warning. You are lost within twenty minutes.

The things that live in the Black City — if live is the right word — begin to take an interest in you around midnight. They do not attack directly. They simply get closer. And closer. And closer still.

When the grey dawn finally comes, you are somewhere near the center of the city, sitting with your back against a wall, and the things are still there, just out of sight. The Marsh Riders outside seem almost welcoming by comparison.

There is no good ending to be found in the heart of the Black City of Wizards.

You shinny up the mill wheel's housing, find the counterweight rope — old hemp, nearly rotted through — and saw it with your belt knife. The Zamoran borderlands are rolling scrub country — brown and grey and yellow, with the distant walls of Arenjun just visible on the southern horizon. You are perhaps half a day's walk from the city.

You make camp in a hollow out of the wind, and that night the dream is different.

Vammatar does not point this time. She walks toward you across a white plain, step by measured step, her feet leaving no marks. Her eyes are fixed on yours. When she speaks, you hear it the way you hear sounds underwater — distorted, delayed, but unmistakably clear: "I know where you are, Cimmerian. I am coming." She raises one hand.

You wake with frost on your bedroll in the middle of summer. The air around your camp smells faintly of green lightning.

She is not here yet. But she is close enough to send her attention ahead of her. You need to reach Arenjun before her body follows.

Dawn comes and with it the camp wakes. Every guard is alert; there is no moment of sleepy inattention you can exploit. You attack anyway, because a Cimmerian does not back down. You reach the ritual circle and your hand is almost on the staff when the sheer weight of numbers reaches you.

The staff hits the iron stand and shatters.

It does not break the way iron should break. It comes apart in pieces that dissolve as they fall, and the sound is wrong — not a metallic ring but something sustained and high and increasingly distant, like a scream receding at speed. The runes go dark. The smell of Hyperborea vanishes from the air. The talisman in your pocket goes very still.

Somewhere to the north, Vammatar the Deathless feels her anchor in the mortal realm dissolve. She is not dead — she is Deathless, and that name means something. But she cannot manifest south of the Hyborian peaks without the staff. Not for years. Not until she builds another.

The sentries heard the sound. You are moving before they turn.

You walk away from Vammatar's camp in the grey pre-dawn with nothing but your pack, the old Cimmerian sword, and the knowledge that the road south is finally, genuinely open. The walls of Arenjun are on the horizon. Behind you, there is only confusion and a camp full of soldiers whose employer has just lost her power of pursuit.

You are seventeen years old, and you are free.

Zamora is waiting.

Arenjun. The Maul.

The city smells like a knife fight in a perfume shop. The Maul district is the part of Arenjun that does its best business after dark: narrow streets, torchlit tavern fronts, the sound of argument and laughter and once in a while something less pleasant. There are more armed people per square yard than you have ever seen in one place, and approximately half of them appear to be doing something illegal.

You feel almost at home.

The blind beggar's words are still in your ears — Tonight you will find adventure in Arenjun — and the city is already making good on the prophecy. Three directions are calling.

To the south, torchlight flickers up from below street level — some kind of underground precinct. There is chanting. The Temple District begins somewhere down there.

To the east, a tower rises above the rooftops: ancient, windowless for the first five stories, a single light burning at the very top. The Tower of the Sorcerer, they call it in the taverns. Nobody knows his name.

To the west, a tavern sign — the Curved Knife — swings over a lit doorway, and the smell of actual food comes out of it.

Something makes you hesitate. The staff is cold and wrong in your hands, but it is also unmistakably powerful — and perhaps there is a use for that power you have not thought of yet. You take it and run.

The torchlight leads you down a stair, through a short passage, and into a space that was not always underground — the ceiling has the curved shape of an old cistern, repurposed. The chanting is louder here and has a rhythm to it that your body does not enjoy.

You find a crack in the ancient cistern wall and look through.
