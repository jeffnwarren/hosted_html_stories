THE TOMB OF CRATH VELDRUN

An Adventure of Choice

You are Maren Ashfall — former sergeant of the Ironwall Company, sole survivor of the Ruin at Veth Bridge.

That engagement, three years past, has followed you the way bad weather follows certain ships. You gave the order to fall back when you should have held. Fourteen men died when the line broke. The inquiry cleared you; the men who signed the papers didn't have to live with the faces.

You have been moving since, taking work where it comes. You are moderately good with a sword, very good at reading danger, and useful in tight places. You speak two languages, read a third, and know enough about old locks to get through most of them. These qualities are worth money in most cities.

Dresh-Amul is a city of white stone and dry heat, built on the ruins of something older. The locals mention what lies beneath the city in the same voice they use for weather and misfortune. You arrived six days ago. Your purse has grown thin.

When a man named Orel finds you at the Carved Door tavern and says that Lord Casvan Dresh is looking for someone capable and discreet, you have little reason to refuse. Good luck — and good judgment.

You had a contract. You honored it. That used to feel like enough.

The road is long. You ride with the payment, and with the memory of a voice through stone that asked for nothing except to be remembered. You find, in the miles that follow, that you keep telling the story — to innkeepers, to other travelers, to anyone who asks where you've been. A king who governed well, you say. 112

There is a blacksmith's forge in the Old Quarter whose owner, for sufficient coin, asks no questions. You feed the Kingstone into the fire.

It takes longer than stone should. The material resists in a way that ordinary rock doesn't, holding its shape as the heat builds. You add more fuel. You wait. After a long time, it softens — and then fractures — and then something that is not a sound, but which you hear anyway, moves through the forge like a breath long held finally released.

The carved face is gone. What remains is slag, cooling to nothing.

You stand there until the forge dies down.

You hope he was what he said he was. You hope the thing you did was the right thing. You have no way to know — that, you are beginning to understand, is usually how it goes with the decisions that matter. The ones that can't be undone.

You leave the city that evening. Casvan's men are not looking for you yet; they will be by morning, when the Kingstone doesn't arrive. You ride north on the coin from your advance payment and don't look back.

The road is long. You've had worse company than your own conscience.

THE END

You emerge from the Tombs in the long shadow of late afternoon, Crath Veldrun walking beside you. He is unsteady on the steps but does not ask for help, and you don't offer it.

He stops when he sees the city — the white buildings, the changed skyline, the Temple with his name on it. He stands there for a long time, very still, looking.

"Three hundred years," he says quietly. He says it the way you'd say the name of a bridge you once crossed and never thought you'd see again.

"Are you going to try to reclaim it?" you ask.

He looks at you. His eyes are gray, very old, very tired, and surprisingly alert. "I'm going to walk through it first," he says. "See what it has become. Decide whether what I would build is better than what exists, or merely different." He pauses. "That seems like the responsible approach."

He extends his hand. "You are free to go. You owe me nothing. I owe you considerably more than I can repay today — but I have some experience with the long game." A pause. "Go north. I will find you."

You shake his hand. His grip is cold from the stone but firm. Then he turns and walks down into the city, and you watch him go, and then you walk in the opposite direction, toward the gate.

Three weeks later, a letter finds you at an inn three hundred miles north. It contains a bank draft for a sum that makes you sit down abruptly. The signature is in archaic formal script that you have to have translated by the innkeeper's daughter, who studied letters.

It reads: Crath Veldrun, First of That Name, in gratitude and in good faith.
