THE HOLLOW KING

An Endless Quest™-style Adventure

Veth the Hollow has lived in Darkspire Castle for three years now, and the valley has learned to leave him alone. He is a lich—a wizard who refused death and paid the price, his body preserved beyond its natural span by old magic and older will. He does not bother the villages and the villages do not bother him, and there has been a kind of peace.

That peace ended last night.

Your father Aldas is the finest sage in the valley, and his spellbook contains knowledge accumulated over forty years. Veth broke into his study, drained him nearly to nothing, and took the book. Not for its spells—a lich already knows more than most spellbooks contain. For one specific ritual: the waking of Kardrath, an ancient vampire lord sealed in the vault beneath Darkspire two hundred years ago. Veth has been searching for that ritual for a long time.

You are Mira, your father’s apprentice: half-elf, fifteen, trained in the early stages of the craft, and gifted with something your father always called “your particular talent” without ever quite explaining it. You have prophetic dreams. You woke this morning from a dream that showed you exactly what was happening, and you woke to find it already done.

Your familiar Pip—a small, bad-tempered hedgehog of advanced years—is on your shoulder. It says: “Now. Go now.”

Darkspire is twelve miles north. Bram, the hedge-witch, is two miles east. Your father is on the floor of his study, still breathing, but not for much longer if the book is not recovered.

1

You wake before dawn to the sound of Pip screaming.

Not in words—the hedgehog manages only fragments of language, short utterances that cost it enormous effort and come out sounding like words spoken through a door—but in the high, continuous alarm-sound it makes when something is genuinely wrong. In three years of keeping Pip you have heard it exactly twice.

You smell smoke and something chemical, and then you are out of bed with your boots on before the second thought catches up to the first.

Your father Aldas is on the floor of his study, his arms still outstretched toward the window as though reaching for something he could not quite grasp in time. He is alive—just—his breathing shallow and labored, the color gone from his face in a way that has nothing to do with ordinary illness. The spellbook’s shelf is empty. The iron clasp that held it lies on the floor, torn loose.

You know before you look. The dream you had three nights ago—which you dismissed as fever-imagination—reassembles itself behind your eyes with perfect clarity: Darkspire Castle on the hill north of town, and inside it, in a room below ground, something with hollow eye sockets and dead hands turning the pages of a book you recognize.

Veth. The lich who moved into Darkspire three years ago. The valley has learned to stay out of his path. Apparently that was a mistake.

Pip pulls at your sleeve. “Now. Go now.”

The hedge-witch Bram lives two miles east along the creek road. She knows more about Darkspire and the things that live in it than anyone else in the valley. The detour would cost you an hour.

2

You ride north in the dark, Pip in your saddlebag with its nose out, testing the air.

An hour before Darkspire you find the road blocked. Four shapes stand in the moonlight ahead—roughly human, dressed in the remnants of what they were buried in, carrying torches in hands that do not flinch from the flame. They move with the slow deliberate purpose of things that do not tire and do not feel cold. Veth’s servants, raised from the old graveyard at the edge of his property. They were watching the road before you arrived. They have been watching it since the night of the theft.

They are spreading to flank you. This is not random. They knew you were coming, or were watching for anyone who might.

Pip presses flat against your neck and says nothing, which is worse than anything it might say.

3

Bram answers before you knock. She is a small, gray, economical woman who does everything with the efficiency of someone who has been doing it for decades and stopped caring about appearances somewhere along the way. She listens to what happened in twenty seconds of your telling and disappears into her back room without speaking.

She returns with a clay sphere the size of a fist, its surface carved over every inch with characters too small to read by lamplight.

“Sunstone,” she says, pressing it into your hands. It is warm—warmth that travels up your arms and does not stop. “Three weeks to make one. Don’t drop it.” She shows you the word carved into the base. “Hold it toward him and speak the word. Liches hold themselves together with will and old magic. The sunstone unravels the will part.”

Then she sits down, and her expression changes.

“Mira. There’s something else.” She folds her hands. “Veth didn’t take your father’s book for its spells. He’s known more than that book contains for a hundred years. He took it because it holds a specific ritual. There is something in the vault beneath Darkspire—an ancient vampire lord named Kardrath, sealed in his coffin two centuries ago by his own generals when they finally turned against him.”

She looks at you steadily. “Veth wants to wake him. He believes Kardrath will reward whoever raises him.”

One more thing: the priests who built the chapel above Kardrath’s vault left a Mirror of Dawn on the altar. A ritual object, shaped to focus dawn’s first light. Their insurance—left so that if Kardrath were ever accidentally awakened, whoever found him would have a means of sealing him again permanently.

“I don’t know if it’s still there,” she says. “But if the King wakes while you’re inside that castle, you will want to find out.”

Darkspire is not impressive so much as it is wrong: a squat, graceless keep on a low hill, iron-dark stone without ornamentation, four towers at the corners and a gatehouse in the center of the south face that has not been maintained by anyone who cared about it living. 5

The portcullis comes down before you are clear of the arch. The weight of it is certain and absolute.

The cage is very dark. Pip makes a sound you have never heard before—low, continuous, the sound of an animal in a place it cannot escape and knows it cannot escape. It says nothing in words. There is nothing in words for this.

Something in the tower opens. Something begins to walk down the stairs, unhurried, its footsteps deliberate in the way that things are deliberate when they have no reason to hurry.

You never learn what Veth does with the spellbook, or whether Kardrath wakes, or whether there is anyone who comes for your father in time.

17

Someone has reorganized this library according to a logic that makes sense only to Veth. The section he has left untouched is the historical record: too dull for a lich, apparently, whose relationship with history is personal rather than academic.

One of those histories is about Kardrath. You read quickly, standing in the cold with Pip on your shoulder, turning pages with fingers that are already going numb.

Kardrath ruled this entire valley two hundred years ago. When his generals finally turned against him, they sealed him in the vault beneath this castle—his own castle, originally—using a Mirror of Dawn that the priests who consecrated the chapel above had left specifically for this purpose. The mirror focuses dawn’s first light into a concentrated ray. Directed at Kardrath’s coffin from outside, it will open the crystal seal permanently. Directed at the coffin from inside the vault, with the intention of sealing rather than opening, it will close it permanently.

The priests knew someone might need this information in a hurry. They wrote it down.

Inside the back cover of the history there is a rough map of the lower level. It shows a chapel in the east wing, off a sealed passage.

21

The study is cold and methodical—the workspace of someone who has had centuries to develop habits and has developed them thoroughly. Every surface has a purpose. Every object is precisely where it was placed and has not moved since.

Your father’s spellbook is not here. Veth keeps it with him.

But his notes are here, and you read the relevant pages in sixty seconds of controlled fear: the ritual requires a concentration of focused light to break the crystal seal on Kardrath’s coffin. The Mirror of Dawn—which Veth has searched for and cannot find—is the only object capable of providing this. Without it the ritual will fail, or take decades longer than Veth has patience for. With it, the seal will break in minutes.

The notes also say where the mirror is. The chapel in the east wing, off a sealed wooden door. Veth has not found it because he searched the chapel before re-sealing the door and did not recognize what he was looking at.

Pip has been completely silent since you entered. Now it says: “Here.”

You turn.

A small brass cage has closed around Pip. It is magical—the bars glow faintly, and Pip’s small claws make no impression on them. A spring-release trap, set in the floor, triggered by something roughly hedgehog-sized. Veth thought of this specifically. He knew a spellcaster might come with a familiar and he planned for it.

Pip looks at you through the bars. Its expression is, as usual, difficult to read.

If you stop and work to open the cage and free Pip, turn to page 23.

If you leave Pip in the cage and go for the Mirror of Dawn, turn to page 24.

The pitch-and-wax seal cracks when you push the door, releasing the held cold of a room that has not breathed since it was shut. The passage beyond is old chapel construction—arched ceiling, carved devotional stonework worn almost smooth, oil lamps in iron brackets that light as you enter, one after another, triggered by the presence of a living person. Someone built that enchantment into the lamps deliberately. They wanted whoever came here to be able to see.

At the end, on a stone altar, an object covered in heavy undyed cloth.

You pull the cloth back. A mirror: oval, six inches across, framed in silver that has not tarnished despite two centuries in a sealed room. When you hold it in the lamplight the glass catches and does something to the light—amplifies it, focuses it, warms it from ambient glow into something more purposeful, something that has direction and intent. It is not comfortable to look at directly.

The Mirror of Dawn. Exactly where the history said it would be, and precisely where Veth was unable to find it because Veth looked at it with dead eyes and saw only an old mirror and not what it contained.

You wrap it carefully in your cloak and take it.

The hollow of his eye sockets gives the impression of vast depth—not emptiness, but something very far away and very old looking out from a long distance.

The air around him distorts slightly, the way air distorts above heated stone, but cold. His lips do not move, but something in the room does: a change in the quality of silence, as though the silence itself is being reshaped by what he is reciting.

Through the vault door, through the stone beneath your feet, you feel it: a slow rhythmic pulse, like a heartbeat belonging to something enormous that has been still for two centuries and is beginning, very gradually, to wake.

The sunstone burns like holding a second sun. The word leaves your mouth and the light leaves the stone and Veth stops reading mid-syllable.

The scream is not sound exactly—it occupies the frequency that sound occupies, the space where sound should be, filled suddenly with something that has no name in any language you have been taught. The spellbook falls. The distortion in the air around Veth collapses inward. The hollow eye sockets fill, briefly, with something that might be recognition—or might be only the projection of what you need to believe—before the light reaches them.

Then he is gone. Not destroyed the way living things are destroyed. Unmade: the will that held him together released all at once, two centuries of preserved malice dispersing into cold air like smoke from a fire that has finally run out of fuel.

Your father’s spellbook lies on the floor. You pick it up.

The vibration in the stone is stronger now. A slow rhythmic pulse, deep and patient, like something very old that has been waiting a very long time and has almost finished waiting.

There are no good options when you have nothing useful and everything depends on getting past something that could destroy you with what amounts to focused intent.

Except.

Pip drops from your shoulder to the floor without a sound. It walks directly toward Veth, making a high continuous alarm-sound that is not quite language and has never been mistaken for anything other than what it is: the sound of a small animal making itself heard on purpose.

Veth lowers the book.

An undead wizard who set spring traps specifically for familiars does not ignore a familiar walking calmly toward him in his own corridor. He looks at Pip. His attention—all of that terrible ancient focused attention—moves to the small hedgehog walking toward him across the stone floor.

30

There is a particular quality to the attention of something that has been dead for a very long time and is accustomed to being the most dangerous thing in any room it occupies.

Veth looks up from the spellbook.

He does not speak. He does not need to. The distortion in the air moves toward you and the temperature drops with the speed of a door slamming, and you have nothing in your hands and nothing in your reach that has any hope of changing what is about to happen.

31

You run.

The vibration in the stone deepens as you reach the stairs, as you cross the ground floor, as you push through the gate and into the cold air outside. Behind you, Darkspire makes sounds it has not made in two hundred years: stone on stone, something very old shifting its weight for the first time in a very long time.

You reach the road before you stop. Your horse is where you left it. The spellbook is in your arms. The sunstone is spent—cold now, inert, its purpose fulfilled.

Veth is gone. The ritual, as far as you could tell, was not complete when you destroyed him. Whether Kardrath will continue to wake without the ritual being finished, or whether Veth’s unmaking broke the process—you don’t know. You may find out soon. You may be making this ride again before the week is out, with more preparation and better tools.

Your father will recover. Bram said the theft drains the owner proportionally to the book’s power, and your father is strong. He has been strong before.

If Pip is with you, it is on your shoulder, its nose working even now, testing the morning air. It says nothing for a long time. Then it says: “Good.” That is more than it usually says. You decide it counts.

If you left Pip in the cage: the cage was magical, and its maker is gone. Whether Veth’s destruction unraveled his traps or whether the cage was designed to open eventually on its own—you will not know until you go back. You tell yourself this is not the same as knowing Pip is dead. You keep moving. You keep telling yourself this.

32

The vault beneath Darkspire is a natural cave enlarged by old construction, its walls carved with devotional stonework from a period you recognize from your father’s books as two or three centuries before the current calendar. At the center, a coffin of dark stone sealed with a crystal layer that has been glowing for two hundred years and is glowing more brightly now, the light in it rising in slow waves.

The coffin lid is moving. Not dramatically—a fraction of an inch, then stillness, then another fraction. The seal is not broken yet. There is still time. But not much.

You understand, holding the Mirror of Dawn in the vault with Kardrath’s coffin in front of you, exactly what the history said about its purpose: directed at the seal with the intention of closing rather than opening, dawn’s focused light will reinforce the crystal permanently.

You need one still moment. Just one. The mirror feels very light and very certain in your hands.

If you hesitate, turn to page 35.

33

The vault beneath Darkspire is larger than you expected, and it is lit from inside the coffin, and the light is growing.

You have the spellbook, or you have nothing, and there is no ritual in either that you can perform in thirty seconds against something sealed for two hundred years and now unsealing. There is nothing in your hands and nothing in this room that changes the direction this is going.

The coffin lid moves.

On the other side of the vault door, in the corridor, somewhere: Pip. In a cage, or free from it, or something else entirely. You will not know. You are not going back through that door. You are not sure you are going anywhere at all.

Whatever happens next in this vault, and in this valley, and to the spellbook you came so far to recover—you are not equipped to be the one who stops it. Not tonight. Not like this.

34

Dawn’s light through the Mirror is nothing like what you expected. It is not a beam. It is more like a fact—the vault filled with it in an instant, purposeful and absolute, and the crystal seal responds immediately, and the light from the coffin shifts in character from the pale rising glow of something waking to the cold, steady white of something held.

The lid stops moving.

The silence that follows is the loudest thing you have heard in your life.

If Veth was destroyed: your father’s spellbook is in your arms or nearby on the floor, and the ritual is incomplete, and Kardrath will not wake again by any working that Veth began. You take the Mirror of Dawn from the vault. It seems right that it should not be left here unguarded again.

If you slipped past Veth to reach this vault: when you emerge, the corridor is empty. The distortion in the air, that cold shimmer of ancient presence, is gone. He abandoned Darkspire. Without Kardrath’s promised reward, without the ritual, without whatever future he had built in his imagination around this moment—two centuries of purpose evaporated in the time it took you to use the mirror. Where he went you do not know. The spellbook is on the floor where he dropped it when he fled. You take it.

If Pip is with you: outside Darkspire the morning is fully established, pale and cold and entirely ordinary. Pip sits on the pommel as you ride south, looking aggressively satisfied, its nose working. You think about saying something. You decide against it. Some things are understood between a person and their familiar without being spoken, and this is one of them.

If you left Pip behind: Veth’s study is on the way out. You go back for it. The brass cage is on the floor, and it is open—whether because Veth’s destruction unraveled his traps or because the cage was designed to release eventually, you don’t know and it doesn’t matter. Pip is in the corner of the study, curled tight. Then, as you stand there, it uncurls. Runs its claws along its spines. Looks at you. Climbs onto your hand without being asked.

It does not say anything. Neither do you.

Your father will recover. The valley will be safe. The Mirror of Dawn is in your pack, and the spellbook is in your arms, and Darkspire’s shadow falls behind you as you ride south into the morning.

The moment passes. One moment of stillness, and you do not use it.

The coffin opens.

Kardrath stands, and the vault fills with a cold that has nothing to do with temperature—the cold of something that has had no warmth for two centuries and has learned not to miss it. His eyes find you immediately. Of course they do. You are the only living thing in the room.

The Mirror of Dawn hits the stone floor. It does not break—it is very well made, and very old, and it was built to survive exactly this kind of failure. It will be here for whoever comes next, if anyone comes next, if they come in time and know what to do with it and do not hesitate.

You hope someone does.

THE END
