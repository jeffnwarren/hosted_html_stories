THE FERRYMAN

An Adventure of Choice

You are Aldric, ferryman on the Awen. The river is a border — the eastern bank is the Realm, the western bank is the Confederacy, and the two have been in a cold sort of war for eleven years that nobody officially acknowledges. The Confederacy has its own customs officers and its own patrol pattern. The Realm has its own officials. You are neither.

You run the ferry three times a day: dawn, midday, and dusk. You carry anyone who pays the fare. You don't ask what people are carrying or why they're crossing. You carry no weapons on the boat — a rule you made in your first year and have kept since, because a ferryman with a weapon becomes something else eventually, and you know what that something is.

In eleven years of operation, the three rules have been tested regularly and held every time.

Tonight there is a knock at your door at midnight. The woman on your step is young, well-dressed in clothes that have been slept in, and she has a leather bag over one shoulder. In her hand she holds gold — enough for a month's passage, pressed into her palm and offered before she has said a word. On the far bank, through the fog, you can see torches moving in a pattern you recognize.

She comes in out of the doorway and stands at the edge of your lamplight, wet from the fog. The leather bag over one shoulder. The gold still in her hand — she hasn't put it away. Her name, she says, is Cael. She is a courier. She has been walking since yesterday morning.

The soldiers on the far bank are Confederacy border patrol. She says they've been moving south to north, sweep-pattern, since the second hour. Looking for someone. You know the sweep pattern: three hours to complete, and they don't double back.

"Who are you carrying for?" you ask.

She looks at you steadily. "Does it matter for the crossing?"

In eleven years of running this ferry, no one has answered a question of yours with a better question. You tell her the rule: you carry anyone who pays, you ask nothing. She's already paid. "I'm asking anyway," you say.

She sets the bag on your table. "Messages," she says. "I carry messages. I don't read them."

"It's midnight," you say.

"I know."

"I don't run at midnight. It's not a rule — it's engineering. The channel markers aren't lit. The river is a different thing in the dark." You pause. "I've done it before. Three times in eleven years."

"What were the circumstances?"

"A woman in labor. A flood warning. A fire."

She doesn't say: and this. She doesn't have to. The gold is on the table. The torches on the western bank are still moving, and you have, at most, two hours before the sweep pattern brings them to the crossing point.

She answers without hesitation — which tells you she has already decided how much to say and that the calculation came out in your favor.

"The messages are for the Weavers," she says. "You know who they are."

You do. Everyone in the border towns does. The Weavers are a network of trade reformers on the Confederacy side who want to renegotiate the levies that have made crossing unprofitable for small merchants. They are not violent. They are, to certain people in certain offices, intensely threatening.

"The patrol isn't routine," she says. "Someone reported me. They know I'm crossing tonight. They don't know where."

The gold on your table is more than a courier makes in a month. Someone is paying for this crossing specifically.

"If they find you," you say.

"The messages name names. Yes."

1. Take her across. Turn to page 13.

2. She can stay the night, but the messages go in the fire first. Turn to page 21.

3. This is beyond a standard crossing. She needs to find another way. Turn to page 25.

The river at midnight is cold and dark and loud. You're forty yards from the eastern bank when you lose sight of it. The western bank doesn't exist yet in the darkness ahead. The Awen runs faster after sunset, or seems to — something you've never verified but believe. The oars are loud.

Cael sits in the bow with the bag under her coat and says nothing. There is no moon. The torches on the western bank are east of your position — you left south of the standard crossing point deliberately — but one of them has stopped moving. A stopped torch is a watcher.

Midway across, it begins to turn in your direction.

1. Row hard. Get to the western bank before the watcher reaches the landing. Turn to page 29.

2. Ship the oars. Let the current take you south, into the dark stretch below the crossing point. Turn to page 33.

She accepts without argument. She follows you to the boathouse and sits in the corner you point to — cold stone, river smell, old rope — and you give her the spare blanket and the small lamp turned low. Through the door you can see the torches on the western bank still moving. Methodical. Patient.

You go back to your house and don't sleep. At the fourth hour the torches go out. At the fifth hour dawn arrives, gray and cold and ordinary.

You run the morning crossing at first light. Standard fare. She sits in the bow with the bag under her coat and doesn't look at the western bank as it approaches. When the boat grounds she steps off and walks into the tree line without stopping, without looking back.

You don't know what's in the bag. You don't know whether it's the same bag she arrived with or whether she spent the night doing something with its contents. You didn't ask. That was the arrangement.

You row back east in the morning light. The Awen runs ordinary. The crossing was routine.

21

She looks at the fire for a long moment. Then she opens the bag, removes the oilskin, takes out the messages. There are seven of them — you count without meaning to.

She puts them in the fire herself. It takes a while. When it's done she folds the oilskin precisely and puts it back in the bag.

"Tomorrow?" she asks.

"Dawn," you say.

She sleeps in the boathouse. The torches on the western bank cycle through their pattern and extinguish. At dawn you take her across: standard crossing, standard fare. The western bank is quiet. She steps off the boat.

She says nothing. There is nothing to say. She had things to deliver and now she doesn't have them. Whether whoever was waiting for those messages has another way of receiving them is not something you know, and not something she offers you.

The crossing was safe. Something was exchanged for that safety. Whether it was worth it is not a calculation you were asked to make.

She picks up the bag. She says nothing — not an accusation, not a goodbye. She walks out of your door and down to the riverbank, which is the only direction she can go.

In the morning there is no crossing. In the morning there is a woman's body at the waterline, south of your dock, where the river eddies against the reeds. The patrol found the crossing point.

You don't know what she was carrying or for whom or whether any of it mattered in the way she thought it did. You know: she came to your door. You know: she had gold in her hand. You know what you said.

The rule is the rule. The rule says you carry anyone who pays. She paid. You didn't carry her.

There will be other people at your door. You will carry them. The rule will apply. The thing you are carrying now — the specific weight of last night — is not the rule and not the ferry and not something the river can resolve. It's something else. You'll find out what it becomes.

29

You row. The oars are too loud and your breathing is too loud and the darkness is the kind that comes from moving fast through something you can't see. The oars bite and pull and bite and pull.

The western bank arrives: reeds first, then mud, then the landing. She's out of the boat before it fully grounds, moving low and fast. You hear her feet on the bank and then nothing.

The torch reaches the landing as you push off.

"Ferryman." A young voice. "Who did you bring across?"

The current takes you east. You're already ten yards out, twenty. The dark between you and the western bank is solid.

"I carry anyone who pays," you say. "I ask nothing."

He says something else, but you're too far out to hear it clearly. You row home in the dark.

Whether she reached wherever she was going. Whether the messages reached whoever needed them. Whether the young soldier reported you and whether that will come back to you. These are the questions your rule was never built to answer. You know your side of the river. You know the water between.

THE END

33

You ship the oars and let the river take you. It runs south-southwest and in the dark you move fast — further than intended, past the crossing point, past the place where you can see the torchlight at all. The fog rises off the water. You are invisible in it.

The western bank arrives as reeds: sudden, solid. You ground the boat in mud and she climbs out without being told, wades to the bank with the bag held over her head, stands in ankle-deep water on the far shore.

She turns back once. You can see the pale of her face in the darkness.

"Keep south," you say. "There's a track behind the reeds. Three miles south is Ardwen village."

She nods. She's gone.

You pole back out into the current and work upstream to your dock. Your boots are wet. Your hands are cold. The Awen runs quiet.

She made it across. However she planned to use what was in that bag — whether the people she carried it for were worth the carrying — that was always going to be her side of the river. You know your side. You know the water between.

THE END
