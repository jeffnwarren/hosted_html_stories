How to Read This Book

You are the hero of this story. Your choices decide what happens next.

When you reach the end of a numbered section, you will be told which sections to turn to. Do not read straight through; jump to the section the book sends you to. Some choices lead to glory. Some lead to ruin. There are many endings. Few are happy. One or two are.

Begin at section 1.

1

The wind that comes down from the Iron Crags this autumn carries something wrong in it — a thin, sour smell, like old iron left too long in the rain. You smell it now as you climb the stone stairs of Master Pentegarn's tower, two steps at a time, a clay jar of broth balanced in your hand.

You are sixteen winters old. You have lived in the village of Brookmere all your life — first in the orphanage by the river, then in this tower as Master Pentegarn's apprentice. He found you when you were eight, picking berries you couldn't name in the woods that even the old men wouldn't enter. He said you had "a way with seeing things others miss."

At the top of the stairs, the door is already open.

"Come in, child. Quickly."

Master Pentegarn lies in his bed by the window, propped on a mountain of pillows. He looks shrunken inside his black robe, his white beard gone thin and yellow. But his eyes — his eyes are the same as they have always been: brown and bright and impossibly, terribly old.

"Set the broth down. I won't drink it. Sit."

You sit on the wooden stool beside his bed.

"There is a story I have not told you," he says. "There is no more time. Listen now."

Turn to 2.

2

"Three hundred years ago," Pentegarn says, "I was court wizard to King Aldric, in the Sunken Realm. The maps no longer remember it. It lay east of the Iron Crags, where the Salt Marshes are now."

His thin fingers grip the blanket.

"A creature came up out of the Endless Catacombs beneath the old city. He had been a man, once. A priest of some forgotten order. He had made himself into something that does not die — a lich. We called him Vorthel. He took the city in a single night.

"I was the only one of the king's circle to escape. I bore with me two things. The first was the Mirror of Truth, forged by the dwarves of the Iron Crags in an older age — a mirror that shows what is, not what pretends to be. The second was the Fireseed: a fragment of the sun that fell on the world long ago, when the world was young. Together, the two are stronger than any lich.

"I bound Vorthel into his own tomb, beneath the ruins of his city. I sealed the door with the Mirror's reflection and the Fireseed's heat. And I bound the seal to my own life — so that as long as I lived, Vorthel slept."

Pentegarn's voice grows quiet.

"I am dying, child. I have perhaps a fortnight. When I die, Vorthel will wake. And he is not patient. He has been awake in his dreams for three hundred years, and he is hungry."

Turn to 3.

3

You stare at him. The room feels far away.

"What can be done?"

"The seal must be renewed before I die. The Mirror was hidden, after the binding, in the Mountain of Mirrors — far north, in the Iron Crags. The Fireseed I gave for safekeeping to the only being I knew would still be alive when this hour came: Sszzireen, the Rainbow Dragon, who lairs in the Sunset Cliffs west of here. You must go to both. You must bring them to the ruins of the old city — what travelers now call Nightmare Castle — and use them, together, on Vorthel's tomb door."

"Master, I — I'm a boy. I weed the garden. I copy your books. I'm not a — "

"You are what I have." His brown eyes hold yours. "And you are more than you know. I did not pluck you from the orphanage by accident. There are reasons. There is no time to explain them. There may never be time. Go."

He lifts a thin hand. On his bedside table are three things.

"Take what you will. Then choose your road. There is no road that is safe. Some are less unsafe than others."

To see what is on the table, turn to 4.

4

On the table, side by side, lie three objects:

A short, plain dagger of grey steel, in a leather sheath. "It was your father's," Pentegarn says, watching you. "He served the king with it. It is a soldier's tool, no more, no less."

A small silver locket on a tarnished chain, hinged shut. "This belonged to a queen who had no children. Open it only when you are about to fall, and not before. It will not save you twice."

A wooden flute, dark with age, the size of your palm. "Three notes. Three only. I cannot tell you what each does, because each does what is needed at the moment it is played, and not what you wish. Choose your moment well."

You may take all three. (You will need them all, before this is over.) Pentegarn nods once, and his eyes close, and for a moment you think he has died, but he is only resting.

You stand, gather the three gifts, and step toward the door.

"One more thing," he says, eyes still shut. "You will not go alone. I have already sent word. Your companion will meet you at the bridge at dawn. I let them choose."

To leave the tower and meet your companion at the bridge, turn to 5.

5

You sleep poorly. Before first light you are at the south bridge, the Brookmere fog around your knees, the dagger at your hip and the locket cold against your chest and the flute tucked into your belt. You wait.

A figure comes out of the mist.

Then another. Then a third. Then a fourth.

"He sent word to all of us," says the first, with a short, dry laugh. "Old wizard, old games. He told us to come and let you choose."

They line up along the bridge, four of them, watching you with very different eyes.

The first is a stout dwarf in a leather coat patched with rivets, a hammer slung on his back and a beard the color of red iron. "Drannor, of the High Forge," he says. "My king has gone wrong in the head, and my clan is in pieces, and I am told this errand of yours touches that. I am yours, if you'll have me."

The second is a tall man in a grey kimono with a sword and a shorter sword at his belt, his hair tied back, his face calm as still water. "Hiroshi," he says, and bows. "I was a swordsman of the Eastern Court. I am no longer. I owe Master Pentegarn a debt of years. Today I begin to pay it."

The third is a barbarian, tall and broad and bare-armed in the cold, a great curved blade across his back, his hair black as wet stone. "Kael," he says. "I do not love wizards, but I love liches less. If your road has killing on it, I am useful on it."

The fourth is a young woman in a green cloak, a circlet of holly in her hair, a staff of pale wood in her hand. "Maerwyn, of the True Grove," she says. "There is a sickness in Elderwood. It is the same sickness, I think, that wakes your lich. I would walk with you a while, and learn."

To choose Drannor the dwarf, turn to 6. To choose Hiroshi the swordsman, turn to 7. To choose Kael the barbarian, turn to 8. To choose Maerwyn the druid, turn to 9. To go alone — to send them all home — turn to 10.

6

You give Drannor your hand. He grips it hard enough to bruise.

"Good. The other three would have got you killed in their own clever ways. I will only get you killed in the ordinary way."

He grins under the red beard. The other three nod and turn back into the mist, and they are gone.

Drannor shoulders his pack. "Right. Two roads from this bridge. One goes east, through Elderwood, the long way around the Crags — green trouble, but warm. The other goes north, straight up into my own country — cold trouble, but my people, or what is left of them. Pick."

To take the east road, through Elderwood, turn to 11. To take the north road, into the Iron Crags, turn to 30.

7

You bow to Hiroshi as best you know how. He returns it more deeply.

"It is well. The other three are good people, but the dwarf would have argued with you, the barbarian would have killed too quickly, and the druid — the druid has her own road, though she does not yet know it. We will walk together."

The other three nod and turn back into the mist, and they are gone.

Hiroshi looks at the two roads.

"East through the forest, or north through the mountains," he says. "I am a stranger in this country. The choice is yours. I will fight what comes."

To take the east road, through Elderwood, turn to 11. To take the north road, into the Iron Crags, turn to 30.

8

Kael laughs once, short, and claps you on the shoulder hard enough that you stumble.

"Good. The samurai is a good man but he stops to think, and the dwarf is a good man but he stops to talk, and the witch is a good woman but she stops to listen to trees. I do not stop. Come."

The other three watch him a long moment. The samurai inclines his head, the dwarf snorts, the druid's eyes narrow as if reading him for a sickness. Then they melt back into the mist.

Kael looks at the two roads.

"Forest or mountain?"

To take the east road, through Elderwood, turn to 11. To take the north road, into the Iron Crags, turn to 30.

9

Maerwyn smiles at you, and you feel for a moment that something in the mist has gotten warmer.

"It is well. The others would have been brave for you. I will be careful for you. That is rarer."

The other three nod, the dwarf with a wry tilt of the beard, and they are gone into the mist.

Maerwyn looks at the two roads, and her eyes go far away.

"There is a sickness in Elderwood. I felt it the moment I crossed the bridge. If we go east, we walk into it. We may also walk into help — the True Grove, my own people, are still there, somewhere, if they have not all fallen. The northern road is clean of the sickness, but it has its own troubles."

To take the east road, through Elderwood, turn to 11. To take the north road, into the Iron Crags, turn to 30.

10

You shake your head. "I'm sorry. I have to do this alone."

Drannor scowls. Hiroshi inclines his head. Kael laughs and walks away without a word. Maerwyn looks at you a long, sad moment.

"Be careful, then," she says. "Some of what is ahead cannot be fought with a dagger. Some of it cannot be fought at all."

She presses something small into your hand — a sprig of dried holly, tied with green thread — and turns away.

Then they are all gone, and you stand alone on the bridge, and the world feels very large.

To take the east road, through Elderwood, turn to 11. To take the north road, into the Iron Crags, turn to 30.

11

You take the east road. By midmorning the country has changed. The hedgerows of Brookmere give way to scrub, and the scrub gives way to forest — not the friendly woods you grew up climbing in, but a forest, capital F, an old one, with trunks the width of cottages and branches that lock together overhead like fingers.

This is Elderwood. Even villagers in Brookmere who never travel will tell you the names of three things to fear in Elderwood. You forget what the third one was.

The road becomes a path. The path becomes a suggestion.

Your companion (if you have one) keeps close. "It's gone quiet," says whichever voice it is — Drannor, or Hiroshi, or Kael, or Maerwyn. "Birds. Stop and listen."

You stop. There are no birds.

After a long minute, you hear something else: a thin, off-key piping, very far away, but coming closer. A flute, or a pipe, played by someone who has not learned to play it well. Or perhaps by something whose mouth is the wrong shape.

To leave the path and hide in the brush, turn to 12. To stand your ground on the path, turn to 13. To walk toward the piping, fast, before whatever-it-is gets close enough to react, turn to 14.

12

You step off the path and crouch behind a fallen log thick with moss. Your companion (if any) drops beside you. You make yourselves small.

The piping comes closer. Closer.

Three goblins shuffle into view on the path, in a kind of broken procession. The middle one carries the pipe in its long fingers and blows on it without rhythm. The other two carry, between them, a long bundle wrapped in stained cloth. The bundle moves. The bundle is breathing.

They do not see you. They pass within ten paces of your log. The piping fades back into the trees the way they came.

You wait. Long minutes.

"Whatever was in that bundle was alive," your companion says, in a low voice. "And not goblin. We could follow."

To follow the goblins and try to free their prisoner, turn to 18. To leave the goblins to their business and continue east through Elderwood, turn to 15.

If you are alone, you must choose for yourself. The same choices apply.

13

You stand in the middle of the path, dagger out, and wait.

The piping stops. The forest is suddenly, completely, silent.

Then, very softly, you hear something new: footsteps. Not three sets. Not five sets. Many. All around you. The brush is full of pale eyes.

If you have the wooden flute and play it now, turn to 17. If you do not — or if you do not think to — turn to 16.

14

You break into a run, straight up the path, toward the source of the piping. Your companion (if any) curses and runs after you.

You burst through a tangle of brambles into a small clearing. Three goblins are there, surprised — one with the pipe at its lips, two with a long bundle between them. The bundle is a person, bound and gagged.

The goblins drop the bundle and reach for their hooked knives.

You have surprise. You have momentum. You have, perhaps, two heartbeats before they recover.

To attack the piper first, who looks like the leader, turn to 19. To attack the closer of the two carriers, turn to 20. To grab the prisoner and run, leaving the goblins to chase, turn to 21.

15

You leave the goblins to whatever they are doing and continue eastward. You do not feel good about this. The path winds on. The trees lean closer.

By dusk, the path opens onto a dell with a small stone shrine in it — moss-grown, broken-roofed, but old. There is a face carved on the lintel: a woman with leaves for hair. Maerwyn (if she is with you) bows to it. The others (whichever one is with you, if any) keeps a respectful distance.

You camp in the shrine. You sleep badly. Once in the night you wake and think there is someone standing at the foot of your bedroll, watching you, but when you sit up there is no one there.

In the morning you go on. By midday the path is rising. By evening you can see, through gaps in the trees, the grey shoulders of the Iron Crags.

Turn to 25.

16

The eyes are wolves' eyes — except wolves do not gather in a ring of twenty, and wolves do not stand on their hind legs. The things in the brush are not wolves. They are something the forest has begun to grow in the last few months, since the sickness took root.

You fight. You fight bravely. If you have a companion, they fight beside you, and they fight well.

It is not enough.

You do not feel the bite that ends it. The forest closes over you very gently, like the hands of a mother, and the piping begins again, somewhere far away, and you do not hear the third note.

THE END.

(To begin again, return to 1.)

17

You set the wooden flute to your lips and blow the first note.

It is not the note you meant to play. The flute chooses for itself. What comes out is a single high, clear, cold tone, like a bell rung underwater, and the brush around you flinches. The pale eyes draw back. There is a long, awful silence in which something in the trees decides whether the flute is worse than you are.

The eyes withdraw. The forest goes still — normally still, with bird sounds returning thinly, like a held breath let out.

You take the flute from your lips. It is warm. That was one note, you think. I have two.

The path is clear ahead. You go on. By dusk you are out of the worst of Elderwood and the Iron Crags rise grey before you.

(Mark on a slip of paper: flute notes left: 2.)

Turn to 25.

18

You follow the goblins at a careful distance, slipping from tree to tree. They are not subtle. Their procession crosses a stream and turns down into a hollow where a fire smokes thinly under a sagging hide tent.

There are six more goblins at the camp. Nine in total. Too many to fight head-on, even if you have a companion.

But the prisoner — they have laid the bundle beside the fire, and one of them is sawing at the cloth with a knife. The cloth falls away. Inside is a young woman in green leathers, bound at wrist and ankle, gagged, very still. Her ears are pointed. Her eyes are open and they are furious.

She is an elf. Your companion (if any) has gone tense beside you.

To rush the camp, fight the nine goblins, and free the elf, turn to 22. To wait until dark, then sneak in and free her quietly, turn to 23. To leave her — there are too many goblins, and your quest is more important — turn to 24.

19

You go for the piper. The dagger goes in under its arm, where the leather jerkin has come unlaced, and the goblin folds up around the blade with a noise like a kettle whistling.

The other two come at you. Your companion (if any) intercepts one. The other is yours.

It is a quick, ugly fight. Goblin knives are short and crooked and you have the longer reach. You take a slash on your forearm — it will bleed, it will not kill you — and then your blade is in the goblin's throat and it is over.

The clearing is yours. The bundle on the ground is moving and making muffled noises. Turn to 27.

20

You go for the nearer of the two carriers, who has just dropped the prisoner-bundle. The dagger goes in. The goblin shrieks and falls.

But you have given the piper time. It blows three sharp notes on the pipe — not the wandering piping of before, but a signal — and from the brush around the clearing, six more goblins burst out, knives up.

If you have a companion, the two of you fight back-to-back. It is bad. You take cuts. You give cuts. In the end, two goblins escape into the trees, but the rest are dead, and so are you-not-quite — you are bleeding badly from a slash above your knee.

If you are alone, it is much worse. Turn to 26 if alone, or to 27 if with a companion.

21

You sheathe the dagger, scoop up the prisoner-bundle (it is heavy, and writhing) and run.

Behind you, the goblins shriek and give chase. Your companion (if any) is right behind you, blade out, ready to turn and cover. You crash through brambles. You leap a fallen tree. You burst out into a wider trail and you keep going until your lungs are knives.

After a long, ragged time, you realize the shrieks have stopped. You stop. You set down the bundle. You cut the cords.

Inside is a young woman in green leathers, ears tipped sharply, eyes the color of wet bark. She spits out the gag and says, "You took your time."

Turn to 27.

22

You charge. So does your companion (if any). It is a brave thing. It is a stupid thing.

Nine goblins, with one of them holding a curved bow they had not bothered to show before. The arrow takes you in the thigh on the third stride. The next takes you in the chest.

The forest is very dark, and then it is only dark.

THE END.

(Begin again at 1.)

23

You wait. The light fails. The goblins eat — something — and drink, and argue, and one by one curl up around the fire. Two stay on watch but they are facing outward, not inward.

You and your companion (if any) creep down through the brush. You cut the elf's bonds with your dagger. She does not make a sound. She rises like smoke. She has been waiting.

The three of you (or two of you) slip back up the slope. You are nearly at the treeline when one of the goblins on watch turns and sees you, and yells.

The whole camp boils up. But you have the slope, and the elf, freed, has snatched up a goblin's hooked knife in each hand on the way out, and now you fight a running fight up through the brush.

If you have a companion, you make it out — bloodied, gasping, alive, plus one elf. Turn to 27.

If you are alone, you make it out, barely — but you have taken a bad slash across the back. Turn to 27, but mark: wounded.

24

You turn away. You creep back to the path. You walk east, fast, not looking back. You tell yourself the quest is more important. You tell yourself she would have been killed before you reached her.

Your companion (if any) does not say anything for a long time. They are looking at you differently. Maerwyn, if she is with you, says nothing at all — but you feel, distinctly, that something between you has cooled.

You walk hard, late into the dusk, and you camp at the edge of Elderwood with the Iron Crags visible against the last of the light.

In the night, you dream of the elf's furious open eyes, and the dream does not end when you wake.

(Mark on your slip: shame. It will matter.)

Turn to 25.

25

You wake at first light at the foot of the Iron Crags. The forest is behind you. The peaks rise grey-blue ahead, snow-capped, immense. Somewhere up there is the Mountain of Mirrors.

Before you can begin to climb, you hear a voice — old, cracked, calling — and around the bend in the path comes a hooded figure, leaning hard on a staff, walking unsteadily.

"Travelers. Travelers. Help an old woman? I have lost my way."

She lifts her hood. She is very old, with kind brown eyes.

If you have Maerwyn with you, she steps in front of you and says, very quietly, "That is not an old woman. Do not let her touch you."

If you do not — or if you have no companion — you must decide for yourself. Something about her smile is wrong, but you are tired, and she is small, and the Crags are cold.

To help the old woman, turn to 28. To draw your dagger and stand back, turn to 29. To play the wooden flute (if you still have notes left), turn to 47.

26

There are too many. The piper has called more than you can fight. You take a knife in the side and another across the brow and you go down in the leaf-mould.

The piping starts again, slow and uneven, as the goblins drag your body into the trees.

THE END.

(Begin again at 1.)

27

The elf is Lirien Greenshaft, scout of the True Grove, and she has been a goblin captive for six days and was a day from being eaten. She thanks you grudgingly, then properly. She has a longbow and a quiver and a clear, fierce loyalty, once given.

"I owe you a life," she says. "Where are you bound?"

You tell her about Pentegarn, about the lich, about the Mirror in the Mountain.

She lets out a long breath. "Then our roads run together. There is something I have to show you, and it is on your way. Come."

She leads you, quickly and quietly, eastward through Elderwood by paths only an elf could see. By dusk you are out of the worst of the forest and the Iron Crags rise blue-grey ahead.

Lirien joins you for the rest of the journey. (Mark on your slip: Lirien with party. She has a bow that does not miss, and an eye for traps.)

Turn to 25, but skip past Maerwyn's warning if it does not apply, and continue to the old woman.

28

You step toward the old woman with your hand out. She smiles. Her teeth are very fine, and there are too many of them.

The hand that takes yours is not a hand. It is a thing in the shape of a hand, and it is very cold, and it does not let go.

If Maerwyn is with you, she shouts a word in a language the trees would understand and strikes the not-old-woman across the wrist with her staff, and the grip breaks and the thing shrieks and is gone in a swirl of black leaves. You are saved. (Take a slash on your wrist — dark, slow to heal. Mark: cursed wound.) Turn to 30.

If you are alone, or if your companion is not Maerwyn, the grip does not break. The cold runs up your arm into your chest. The not-old-woman smiles wider. You do not hear what she says next, because you are already falling.

THE END.

(Begin again at 1.)

29

You step back, dagger raised. "Stay there."

The old woman's smile does not change. She comes on, slowly, leaning on her staff. "Don't be afraid, child. I'm only — "

You raise the dagger higher. Your hand is steady.

The smile drops. The kind eyes go flat and silvery, and then she is not an old woman at all but a tall, thin shape in a robe of black leaves, eyeless and toothy, and it hisses and recoils from the steel as if from a torch.

It does not attack. It studies you. Then it turns and is gone into the trees in a swirl of black leaves, and the path is empty.

(Mark on your slip: the Dark Druid knows your face.)

Turn to 30.

30

You take the north road. By midmorning the country tilts upward. The grass thins. Stones the size of cottages lie scattered as if a giant had walked through. By afternoon you are climbing in earnest, switchbacks up scree slopes, the wind beginning to bite.

This is the foot of the Iron Crags, the dwarf country. Your companion (if any) — particularly Drannor — knows the road.

You camp the first night in a hollow under an overhanging rock. You build a small fire of gathered scrub. You sleep. Around the third hour of darkness, your companion (or you, if alone) wake, listening: there is a faint clink of metal on stone, very far below, then closer. Then a voice — guttural, not friendly.

Goblins again. A scouting party coming up the trail.

To put out the fire and hide, turn to 31. To stay where you are and ambush them, turn to 32.

31

You smother the fire. You go silent. You crouch behind the rock.

Five goblins shuffle past below, talking in their low harsh tongue, carrying long crooked spears. They do not see you. They go on up the trail and around a bend, and they are gone.

You let out your breath. Your companion (if any) lets out theirs.

"There will be more behind them," says Drannor (if he is with you), grim. "These ones were a probe. There is something my people are doing that the goblins are very interested in."

You break camp before dawn and continue up. By midmorning you are on a high ridge with the dwarf country spread below: a city built into the mouth of a great cleft in the mountain, banners hanging at the gates, smoke from forges.

Turn to 34.

32

You stay where you are. You let the goblins come around the rock. There are five of them. There are fewer of you.

Your companion (if any) takes one in the throat with a thrown weapon before they know you are there. The other four come on, screaming. You fight in the firelight, and the fight is hot and short.

If you have Drannor with you, he kills two with his hammer and you account for the other two with your dagger. Turn to 33.

If you have Hiroshi, Kael, Maerwyn, or Lirien — same. Turn to 33.

If you are alone, you kill two before they overrun you. The third one's spear takes you in the side. You kill him too, but the other two are on you, and the firelight goes red and then dark.

THE END.

(Begin again at 1.)

33

You roll the goblin bodies down the scree slope so the ravens find them and not the trackers. You patch your wounds. You break camp and continue up.

By midmorning you are on the ridge above the dwarf country.

Turn to 34.

34

The High Forge. The dwarf city in the cleft of the Iron Crags. From your ridge you can see the high gates standing open — standing open, in the middle of the day, with no guards on the walls. Smoke is rising from the forges, but it is the wrong color. It is grey, where it should be black.

"That is wrong," says Drannor (if with you), and his voice has gone strange.

Even if he is not with you, you can feel that something is wrong. The city is too quiet. There are figures moving on the streets, but not many, and they walk slowly, in pairs.

To go straight down to the gates and announce yourself, turn to 35. To circle around the city and find a back way in, turn to 36. To wait on the ridge and watch the city for the rest of the day before deciding, turn to 37.

35

You go down the road to the gates. No one challenges you. You walk into the city of the High Forge unopposed, which has not happened in the eight hundred years of dwarven history.

In the great square inside the gate, dwarves are gathered, perhaps a hundred of them, in a slow circle around a stone block. On the block kneels a dwarf in a torn red coat, hands bound. A tall figure stands over him with an axe.

The tall figure is not a dwarf. The tall figure is a man in black armor, his helm closed, a mirror-bright shield slung on his back.

The Knight of Illusion.

He turns as you enter the square. His helm has no slit for eyes. He sees you anyway.

"How prompt," he says, in a voice like dry leaves. "The wizard's last apprentice. Welcome to my city."

To attack him at once, turn to 38. To demand to know what he has done with the dwarf king, turn to 39. To turn and run, back out the gate, turn to 40.

36

You skirt the city, climbing higher up the cleft. Drannor (if with you) leads you to a small iron door set into the mountain face — a service entrance to the great forges, never used by outsiders, the lock a dwarvish puzzle that no human could solve.

He opens it. You slip inside.

You are in a service tunnel, hot, low-ceilinged, smelling of coal and metal. Dwarvish runes glow faintly along the wall.

You creep deeper into the city. You begin to hear voices — not dwarvish, but human. Two human voices, far ahead, complaining about the heat, complaining about the dwarves, complaining about waiting.

You creep up to a corner and peek around it. Two men in black livery, with long swords, are leaning on a wall outside a heavy iron door. Behind that door, you guess, is something they are guarding.

To rush them, turn to 41. To wait until they move, then slip past them, turn to 42.

37

You wait. You watch. By dusk you have learned three things.

First, the dwarves on the street move oddly — slow, with their heads slightly bowed, as if listening to music only they can hear.

Second, there are humans in the city. Two patrols, in black livery, walking the walls. This is wrong. There are not supposed to be humans in the High Forge.

Third, in the great square inside the gate, a stone block has been set up, and a man in black armor with no slit in his helm stands beside it, and from time to time a dwarf is brought out and made to kneel.

If Drannor is with you, by the second time this happens his face is the color of cold iron and he is shaking. "That is the Knight of Illusion," he says. "He should not exist. He has been a story for a thousand years. He is killing my king's council in front of the city."

To go down at last light and try to free the next prisoner, turn to 44. To circle the city and find a back way in, turn to 36.

38

You charge. The Knight of Illusion does not move.

A Note from the Storyteller

There are many endings to this book. The brightest is 133. The hardest-earned, perhaps, is 136. The ones that come from skipping or shortcutting the trials — 123, 125, 127 — give you a world that is saved but that has a debt against it that someone, someday, will have to pay.

The endings that come from giving up — 115, 117, 128, 61, 62, 63, 64 — are not happy. The endings that come from impatience — 22, 38, 74, 107, 121 — are short.

But you have time. The book is patient. You may begin again at 1 as many times as you like. Each road you walk teaches you something the others do not. The first time through, you will not have the wooden flute's notes left when you need them; the second time, you will. The first time through, you will trust the wrong person; the second time, you will not. By the third or fourth time, you will know the country well enough to choose the brightest road.

That, in the end, is what apprenticeships are for.

Master Pentegarn would be proud.

— The End. (Or the beginning, again.)
