STEEL OF THE MOUNTAIN SHRINE

An Adventure in the Kingdom of Yamato

The Kingdom of Yamato has known peace for a generation. Its cities are full, its roads are maintained, and its Emperor—old now, and fading—has held the throne longer than most of his subjects have been alive.

But peace built on sacred power requires that sacred power to hold. Three relics of the Mountain Shrine at Iron Peak have kept this throne legitimate for a hundred years: the Bell of Heaven, the Jade Mask, and the Mirror of Eternal Snow. Two weeks ago, they were taken.

The thief was Yomiko: the ice spirit of Iron Peak, old before the kingdom was founded, who has watched three dynasties from her mountain and despised them all.

You are Kira, daughter of the swordswoman Hana, trained since childhood in the art of the blade. Two years ago your mother rode north toward Iron Peak on a commission she never explained—and never returned. The Emperor’s summons arrived this morning with a single object: your mother’s katana, Kireha, “cutting leaf,” a blade so old its history predates the hilt.

The Emperor is dying. The relics must be returned before his successor can be crowned, or the throne will have no legitimate foundation when challenges come. And they will come.

You have the sword. The mountain is waiting.

“The relics of the Mountain Shrine,” he says. “Three objects that have kept this throne legitimate for a hundred years. The Bell of Heaven. The Jade Mask. The Mirror of Eternal Snow.” He pauses. “Yomiko has taken them.”

The court goes quiet at the name. Yomiko: the ice spirit of Iron Peak, old before the kingdom was founded, who has watched three dynasties from her mountain and despised them all.

The Emperor opens the box. Inside, on green silk, lies a katana. Your mother’s. Kireha—“cutting leaf”—a blade so old its history predates the hilt. Hana carried it on the day she vanished two years ago, riding north toward Iron Peak on a commission she never explained.

“She came to me before she left,” the Emperor says. “She said that if she did not return, this sword should go to her daughter. And her daughter should go where she had gone.”

You take Kireha. The grip fits your hand as though it remembers your mother’s palm.

There is a question of time. A scholar named Ryuken has spent forty years studying Yomiko’s nature in the eastern hills. He may know something useful. The detour would cost you two days, and the Emperor’s heir grows more impatient by the hour.

33

The Bell’s resonance is still in the mountain stone. You can feel it in the soles of your feet—a low vibration that the rock has been holding since you rang it at the shrine, carrying it down through the peak and into the very walls of this chamber.

You reach for that sound. You speak it aloud, a single syllable, the way Masa described when she explained the Bell’s properties: not a word, exactly, but a shaped breath that asks the stone to carry what it has been holding.

The room picks it up and amplifies it. Yomiko flinches—not dramatically, not the way fire would drive her back, but a structural flinch, the kind that means something in her fundamental cohesion is failing. Her outline shimmers.

Kireha finds the gap. You are fast, and the old blade understands what it was made for, and Yomiko’s form comes apart in a cascade of pale light that turns to frost before it reaches the floor and simply settles there in a thin white layer, like the first snow of autumn.

The Mirror of Eternal Snow goes dark, then clear, then shows you your own face looking back at you—just your face, present and immediate, with no ghosts behind it.

In the gallery behind you, through the wall, you hear the crack of ice, and then another, and then the sound of twenty-two people breathing at once.

When you go back through, your mother is standing free among the others, blinking in the blue light. She looks around, takes stock of the situation with the speed of someone who has been thinking through contingencies for a long time, and then looks at you.

“You rang the Bell,” she says. She sounds unsurprised, as though she always knew you would. “Good.”

Twenty-two people who have been frozen in ice for various lengths of time are not a professional fighting force. But they are angry, and many of them were soldiers and warriors and scholars in their day, and they have been standing in columns of blue ice with their eyes open for a long time, and they have been thinking about what they would do when they got out.

The fight in Yomiko’s sanctum is not elegant. It is loud and close and chaotic, and you take a long gash across your ribs that will leave a scar you will be explaining for years. But Yomiko cannot focus her cold on a single point when there are twenty-two others pressing from every direction, and old spirits, however powerful, are still spirits, and spirits that cannot focus cannot hold their form.

She comes apart in pieces, retreating into the Mirror until the Mirror goes white and then silent and then dark. The temperature in the room begins to rise almost immediately.

Your mother, bleeding from a shallow cut on her forearm, stands next to you in the center of the room. She looks at the Mirror for a long moment, then at you, and something in her expression settles.

“All three,” she says. “You have all three.”

You do.

Yomiko does not give you time to think. The cold crashes into the room like a wave, and you are moving—Kireha drawing a line of warmth through the frozen air—but she is in three places at once, her form refracting across the ice walls in images that are each as solid and present as the original, and there is no single point to press toward.

You try. You fight the way Hana taught you—reading the center of gravity, waiting for the moment of commitment, finding the line between attack and defense. For a few minutes you think it might be enough. It is not. Yomiko is older than the kingdom, older than the mountain road, older than the practice of naming things, and she does not make the kind of mistakes that training is designed to exploit.

The last thing you see clearly is the Mirror of Eternal Snow, and in it your own face, and behind your face, deep in the silver, the face of your mother looking out at you with an expression of grief and helplessness and a terrible, patient recognition.

The cold is very thorough.
