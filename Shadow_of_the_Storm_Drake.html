SHADOW OF THE STORM DRAKE

An Endless Quest Adventure

In Shadow of the Storm Drake, you are Jaimie, apprentice to the wizard Pentegarn. While traveling through the Valley of Greymist, you learn that a powerful sorceress named Malachar has enslaved Stormwing, the great silver dragon who has protected the valley for a hundred years. Worse, Malachar has cast a sleep spell on Pentegarn, leaving you alone with only your friends Fox and Owl to help. The valley's crops are dying. The villagers are afraid. And somewhere inside Malachar's black tower, a dragon waits to be freed.

The choices are yours. Choose wisely — in Malachar's valley, bravery alone will get you killed.

1

The road into the Valley of Greymist is darker than it should be for midafternoon. You are Jaimie, apprentice to the wizard Pentegarn, and you have ridden with him all morning through countryside that grows quieter the farther you go. No birds. No insects. Just a gray sky pressing down on gray fields.

A farmer comes running toward you on the road, waving his arms. His face is white with fear. "Please," he gasps, catching Pentegarn's stirrup. "The sorceress Malachar — she came three nights ago, green lightning and screaming wind. She dragged old Stormwing right out of his mountain cave. He's been our guardian for a hundred years. Without him, everything's dying."

Pentegarn listens gravely. He reaches for his staff to begin a counter-spell — and then his eyes go blank. He crumples from the saddle as if someone has simply switched him off. You barely catch him before he hits the ground.

"A trap," Fox growls, pressing his nose to the air. "Malachar was listening. She put a sleep curse on him the moment he tried to use his power."

Owl lands on your shoulder. "You're on your own, Jaimie," he says quietly. "What will you do?"

The village of Greymist is nearly deserted. Doors are shut. Dogs have gone silent. The only person who will speak to you is an old woman named Betha, who sits spinning outside her cottage with the deliberate calm of someone who has decided not to be afraid of anything.

"She came in a storm," Betha says, her wheel never slowing. "Green lightning and screaming wind. Dragged poor Stormwing right out of his cave — chains of glowing crystal, they were. He's been guardian here since my grandmother's grandmother was a girl."

She lowers her voice. "Malachar draws all her power from her crystal orb. Smash that and she's no more dangerous than a wet cat." She shakes her head. "But getting close enough to do it — that's the trick. The tower's full of traps, and her fire sprites guard every hall."

You thank Betha and turn to go. Then you notice a merchant who has set up a rickety stall beside the empty inn, apparently indifferent to the end of the world around him.

The hidden door is low and iron-bound, almost invisible beneath years of ivy and creeping moss. Fox scratches it open and you squeeze inside, Owl perching on your pack.

The passage is cold and smells of earth and old stone — but not of Malachar's green-lightning magic. Whatever this tunnel is, it predates her by centuries. Fox leads confidently, his nose never stopping. After twenty minutes of careful crawling through darkness that your ring's faint warmth just barely touches, the tunnel opens outward into a stone chamber.

"Under the tower," Fox says, lifting his head. "We're under the tower."

Armed with Betha's knowledge of the orb and the beggar's warning about the pressure stone, you feel more prepared than you have any right to be. You set off down the road toward the tower.

It takes the better part of an hour to reach it. Up close, the tower is even more forbidding — black stone that absorbs light rather than reflecting it, with narrow windows like closed eyes. The main gate is a heavy iron arch.

You look carefully at the ground beneath it. There — slightly darker than the surrounding flags — is a discolored stone exactly where the beggar said it would be.

Fox steps around it delicately. You and Owl follow, giving it a wide berth. Nothing happens. The fire sprites are patrolling the far side of the tower; a side door stands slightly ajar.

You break into a run. But fire sprites are faster than anything that moves on legs — far faster. Before you take ten steps, one of them wheels around with a shriek of burning air. The heat hits you like a wall. You throw up your arms, stumble, and fall.

When you wake, you are far down the road from the tower, singed and exhausted. Owl has dragged you clear of the worst of it, but your hands are blistered and the tower is as sealed as ever. You are in no condition to try again.

Fox sits beside you in silence for a long time. "We should have thought it through," he says, at last.

Inside, Malachar's tower is a maze. Corridors spiral in too many directions. You turn left twice and end up back where you started. You try the staircase and it deposits you on the same floor you left. Fox's ears go back in frustration.

"She designed this to confuse," he says. "Magic doesn't confuse noses, though." He puts his head down and begins to track in slow, deliberate circles, reading the air. After a moment he looks up. "This way. Follow exactly where I go."

He leads you through a sequence of turns that seem impossible — left, right, up half a flight, through a door you did not see, down a hall that should not be where it is — and delivers you, after twenty tense minutes, to a pair of iron doors at the very top of the highest stair. Two fire sprites doze at either side, dimmed to a faint glow.

Fox looks at you. You look at the sprites. They don't wake. You ease the doors open and slip through.

The iron doors open onto an enormous circular chamber. In the center, bound by chains of glowing green crystal, is a creature of breathtaking size: Stormwing, the Storm Drake. His silver scales are dull with captivity, his great eyes half-closed. But his breath still comes in long, slow waves — thunder-quiet and rhythmic. He is alive.

At the far side of the chamber, Malachar stands at an open window with her back to you. A green crystal orb drifts at her side, pulsing softly with captured power.

Fox breathes into your ear: "She hasn't seen us."

You sprint for the orb. But Malachar turns before you take three steps — as if she felt the air move, as if she has been waiting for exactly this. The orb flares brilliant green and a bolt of lightning catches you in the chest, stopping you so completely it is almost gentle.

You wake on the cold stone floor, with Fox pressing his warm nose against your hand and the sound of Malachar speaking to someone far away. The tower is still hers. Stormwing still breathes in slow chains. And there is nothing more you can do today.

Pentegarn once showed you a way to communicate with frightened animals — not words, just a low, steady rhythm, almost like humming. You use it now, barely moving your lips, directing it toward the great silver head.

He cannot speak — the crystal chains silence him — but with enormous effort he extends one forelimb, as far as the chains allow, and points one claw. Not at Malachar. At something to her right: the floating orb.

He's telling you where to strike.

You push the hatch open, step up into the chamber, and bring Pentegarn's staff down on the orb with everything you have.

The sound is less dramatic than you expected — a single, high, clear note, like the ring of a bell — and then the orb bursts apart in a cascade of green sparks that scatter harmlessly across the stone floor and go dark. The chamber plunges into near-blackness for one terrible moment.

Then Stormwing roars.

The chains crack and fall. The great dragon surges upward to his full height, wings spreading against the vaulted ceiling, and the roar shakes plaster from the walls and sends dust cascading down from the rafters. Malachar wheels around — and you can see the exact moment she reaches for her power and finds nothing there. Her face goes very still.

She bolts for the window. Stormwing's tail catches her before she reaches it. She sprawls on the stone floor, stripped of power, looking smaller than you thought possible.

34

By the time Malachar has fled, the valley is already changing. The grey sky thins within the hour. The first green shoots appear in the fields by evening. Fox runs in wide circles in the valley grass, unable to stop grinning. Even Owl does a second loop over the tower, just for the pleasure of it.

Pentegarn wakes from his sleep on the road, confused and indignant in equal measure, and argues for a full ten minutes that he does not need rescuing by his own apprentice. He then listens to your account of everything that happened, and is quiet for a long time.

"Well," he says at last, grudgingly. "You chose correctly at every turn. I suppose I taught you something after all."

Above the valley, Stormwing circles on wide silver wings in the clearing sky. Malachar is gone — but she has escaped, and somewhere she is already beginning again. Still: the valley is saved. The dragon is free. And that is enough for today.

35

The valley changes overnight. The sky clears. The fields wake up. Fox runs in wild circles and cannot stop smiling. Siff the pixie does three victory loops around the tower before settling on Owl's head, which Owl endures with remarkable patience.

Stormwing bows his great silver head until his chin rests on the chamber floor in front of you. He is the largest thing you have ever been in a room with, and the silence he makes is enormous.

"Thank you, young Jaimie," he says, in a voice like weather in the distance. "You showed wisdom where older heroes would have shown only strength. The valley will not forget it."

Malachar is handed over to Betha, who accepts her with a look that makes you profoundly glad you are not Malachar.

Pentegarn wakes in time to hear you tell the whole story. He listens without interrupting once, which is the highest compliment he knows how to give. He says nothing for a long moment afterward. Then he says: "Next term, we work on patience. You already have enough cleverness."

You think that might be the nicest thing he has ever said to you.

THE END
